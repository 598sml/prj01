# Project Presentation Prompts

Your presentation should make it easy for your group to understand
* what problem you are trying to solve,
* what you will actually do,
* and the steps involved

You do not need to have every detail finalized yet. It is completely fine to still be making decisions. The purpose of this session is to help you sharpen the project before you invest too much time in the wrong direction. Try to be concrete, realistic, and honest about uncertainty!

### 1. What is your project question?

Describe the specific question you want to answer, or the task you want to solve.
Try to be concrete. “I want to study neural operators for PDEs” is too broad; “I want to compare FNO and DeepONet for learning solutions of the 1D Burgers equation” is much clearer.

### 2. Why is this a good SciML project?

Explain why this problem is a good fit for scientific machine learning.
What is the scientific component? Where does modeling, simulation, physical structure, or scientific data enter the problem?

### 3. What paper or benchmark will guide your work?

Identify at least one paper, benchmark, or prior project that you can follow or adapt.
This should be something realistic that helps ground your project in an existing method or application.

### 4. What data or simulator will you use?

Explain where your data will come from.
Will you use an existing dataset, generate data from a simulator, or both?
You should also comment briefly on whether the data is actually available and manageable for a class project.

### 5. What exactly will you implement yourself?

Be specific about what part of the project you will build or code yourself.
For example: model architecture, training loop, solver interface, data generation pipeline, evaluation pipeline, ablation study, etc.
Also identify code or packages that you expect to reuse or rely on.

### 6. What are the main risks or bottlenecks?

Identify the biggest challenges or uncertainties for your current plan.
For example: scope may be too large, data may be difficult to obtain, training may be too expensive, or evaluation may still be unclear.

### 7. What is your minimum viable project?

Suppose your ambitious plan turns out to be too much. What is the smallest version of the project that would still be complete and worthwhile?
